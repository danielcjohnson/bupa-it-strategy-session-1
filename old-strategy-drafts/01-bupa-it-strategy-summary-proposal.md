# Bupa IT Strategy Summary Proposal

Status: Draft for review
Date: 6 May 2026
Source context: [strategy session brief](prompts/prompt-it-strategy-session.md)

## Purpose

This proposal sets out a one-page meta view of Bupa's IT strategy for architects and engineers. It is intended to help leaders make choices about enterprise capability investment, platform consolidation, data foundations, and ecosystem growth. It should complement Savannah 27 and C Cubed rather than restate them.

The working proposition is simple: Bupa IT should focus on a small number of enterprise capabilities that improve customer experience, clinical outcomes, and cost discipline across the full care and insurance journey.

## Strategic Frame: Trusted, Connected, Efficient

The proposed strategy brand is **Trusted, Connected, Efficient**.

**Trusted** means that Bupa's digital and data capabilities are safe, consented, clinically responsible, secure, and sustainable by design.

**Connected** means that Bupa can recognise and support a customer, patient, clinician, provider, or partner across journeys, channels, markets, and care settings.

**Efficient** means that Bupa consolidates common capabilities, removes avoidable duplication, automates repeatable work, and measures technology choices against cost-to-serve and value delivered.

This frame translates C Cubed into IT decision criteria: customer value, clinical integrity, and cost effectiveness. It also supports the Savannah 27 shift toward ecosystem growth by making shared enterprise capabilities the foundation for scale.

## Decision Tests

An enterprise capability should be pillorised only when it passes most of these tests:

| Test | Decision Question |
| --- | --- |
| C Cubed value | Does it materially improve customer, clinical, or cost outcomes? |
| Journey reuse | Does it support multiple stages from purchase and enrolment through follow-up? |
| Enterprise reuse | Can it be reused across markets, business units, channels, or partners? |
| Consolidation | Does it reduce duplicated systems, data stores, processes, or controls? |
| Ecosystem readiness | Does it make Bupa easier to connect with providers, partners, platforms, and digital health services? |
| Decision quality | Does it improve the data, controls, or visibility needed for better business decisions? |

If an investment does not pass these tests, it may still be valid locally, but it should not be treated as an enterprise strategic pillar.

## First Capability Footprint

The first hypothesis for the enterprise capability footprint is:

| Enterprise Capability | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Consent and Preferences | Establishes the permission foundation for personalisation, clinical data use, communications, AI, and ecosystem sharing. |
| Single Customer View | Gives Bupa one usable view of identity, relationship, coverage, care context, interactions, and needs. |
| Identity and Access | Creates trusted access for customers, clinicians, employees, providers, brokers, and partners. |
| Customer and Care Orchestration | Connects find, booking, arrival, visit, pay, and follow-up into coherent journeys. |
| Provider and Partner Connectivity | Enables ecosystem growth through reusable APIs, events, directories, referrals, claims, and data exchange. |
| Health, Customer, and Operational Data Products | Moves from reporting to governed, reusable, decision-grade data. |
| Payments, Billing, and Claims Experience | Simplifies moments that strongly shape trust, satisfaction, and cost-to-serve. |
| Clinical Safety and Evidence Governance | Ensures that digital, data, and AI-enabled services remain clinically appropriate, auditable, and safe. |
| Enterprise Observability and Value Management | Makes reliability, adoption, journey performance, unit cost, and value visible enough to manage. |

This is a starting point, not a finished taxonomy. The next step is to test which capabilities deserve enterprise ownership, funding, architecture guardrails, and measurable outcomes.

## Big Leaps Required

Bupa will need to move from application portfolios to enterprise products; from fragmented customer and patient records to consented, shared views; from point integrations to ecosystem-grade APIs and events; from reporting to decision-grade data products; from project funding to capability lifecycle funding; and from local optimisation to group-level cost-to-serve optimisation. These shifts require clearer ownership, common standards, fewer exceptions, stronger data accountability, and explicit trade-offs between local variation and enterprise reuse.

## Principles and Constraints

1. **C Cubed is the portfolio test.** Every strategic IT decision should explain its customer, clinical, and cost impact.
2. **Consolidate the common, differentiate the distinctive.** Shared capabilities should be common by default; local variation should be intentional and justified.
3. **Consent comes before personalisation.** Customer trust depends on clear permission, preference, privacy, and audit foundations.
4. **Journey continuity beats channel optimisation.** Technology should optimise the end-to-end customer and care journey, not only individual touchpoints.
5. **One enterprise capability needs one accountable owner.** Ownership should include roadmap, funding, controls, adoption, value, and lifecycle health.
6. **Data must be productised to drive decisions.** Critical data should have owners, quality measures, lineage, access rules, and defined consumers.
7. **Build for ecosystem participation.** APIs, events, identity, consent, and partner controls should be strategic design concerns, not late integration tasks.
8. **Clinical safety is a design constraint.** Digital, automation, analytics, and AI decisions must be clinically governed where they affect care.
9. **Optimise cost-to-serve, not only IT cost.** Platform choices should consider operational effort, duplication, failure demand, support burden, and customer friction.
10. **Stop funding isolated exceptions as strategy.** Local solutions should prove enterprise reuse, stay deliberately local, or have a path to convergence.

## Decisions This Page Should Drive

This summary should help Bupa decide which capabilities become enterprise pillars, which platforms should be consolidated or retired, which data products and controls become mandatory engineering constraints, which investments should be redirected because they optimise a silo, and which measures determine whether the strategy is working.

The primary outcome is not a better diagram. The outcome is a clearer set of constraints for investment, architecture, delivery, and operational decision-making.

## Guidance Needed

The draft needs feedback on four points before it is ready for circulation:

1. Should the strategy brand stay close to C Cubed, or should it use a more independent frame such as **Trusted, Connected, Efficient**?
2. Which of the proposed enterprise capabilities already have named owners, funding, or active programmes?
3. Should the footprint cover all Bupa markets and business lines, or start with a narrower scope such as insurance, healthcare provision, or digital channels?
4. Are there existing internal principles, regulatory commitments, or clinical governance standards that must be reflected explicitly?
